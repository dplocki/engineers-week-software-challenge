Your boss is a bit of a nerd. Check that – he’s a HUGE nerd. When he sends out team meeting times, he might say something like “the meeting starts at 20 after 8”, which seems normal, but other times he’ll say things like “90 after 8” or “126 till 4”, which gives you pause. When you ask him about this, he says that “20 after 8” means the first time after 8 that the hour and minute hands of the clock make an angle of 20 degrees; “126 till 4” means the closest time before 4 that the hands make an angle of 126 degrees. As your boss walks away snickering, you resolve that you will write a program that will automatically convert his meeting times to more normal, non-nerdy times. That’ll show him!

Input

The input file starts with an integer n indicating the number of test cases. Each test case consists of a single line of the form ‘a after b’ or ‘a till b’, where a and b are integers satisfying 0 ≤ a < 360, and 1 ≤ b ≤

12.All angles are measured starting at the hour hand and moving clockwise until reaching the minute hand (so, for example, at 9 o’clock the hands make an angle of 90 degrees and at 3 o’clock they make an angle of 270 degrees).

Output

For each test case, output the time in the format h:m:s, where h, m and s are the hour, minutes and seconds closest to the given angle and hour and 1 ≤ h ≤ 12. Always use two digits to represent the number of minutes and seconds.

Sample Input

4

20 after 8

126 til 4

180 til 1

0 after 12

Sample Output

8:47:16

3:39:16

12:32:44

1:05:27

Your office space is currently undergoing a rather messy renovation. It seems that every time you step away from your desk, things around you have moved. On top of that, your boss keeps scheduling meetings in a conference room on the opposite corner of the building. When you finally arrive to these meetings, your boss immediately sends you out on a series of tasks that have you running all over the office. Due to the messy renovation, you keep finding yourself lost or at dead-ends.

You decide to make a game based on the renovation. You divide the office into square tiles and create an N by N board, where N is an odd number between 5 and 31 that represents the number of tiles along one edge of your square office building. The starting tile (labelled ‘7’) and the ending tile (labelled ‘8’) are always in opposite corners of the board. The objective tiles (labelled ‘9’) are always in odd rows and columns only. Only tiles along even rows and columns of the board under renovation and movable. The starting tile, ending tile, and all objective tiles are always in fixed locations. Aside from these fixed locations, all other tiles are one of 6 renovation layouts depicted below.

The objective of the game as follows: Starting from your start tile (7), you must reach each of the objective tiles (9) at least once in any order, then reach the end tile (8). When you reach each of the objective tiles, you will “collect” something from the tile.

The green arrows on the tiles depict pathways that you can move along. In order to move throughout the board, you will need to move the tiles in even rows and columns around.

On the edge of the board is an extra tile that is one of the 6 tiles above. You can pick an even row or column of the board to push the extra tile into, rotating the tile if you chose. On the sample boards below, the numbered blue arrows represent the locations where the extra tile can be placed. There are always (N-1)*2 possible positions to place the extra tile. These positions will be labeled numerically starting at 1 for the leftmost moveable tile at the top side of the board and going clockwise to the topmost moveable tile on the left side of the board.

The push action shifts all of the tiles in the row or column and causes a tile to be pushed out the opposite side of the board. The direction of the blue arrows represents the direction that the tiles will be shifted when the extra tile is placed there. Repeat this process by placing the pushed out tile and moving along the green arrows until you have made it to all of your objectives and to the ending tile. Once you reach the ending tile after collecting at each of the objective tiles, you will automatically win the game.

Input

The input file starts with odd integer 5 <= N <= 31 indicating the number of tiles along one side of the square board. On the following N lines are N numbers in base 10 indicating the type of tile in each position of the board and the number of times the tile is rotated clockwise by 90 degrees. The numbers are generated as follows: Each number can be represented with 6 bits in base 2. The 4 most significant bits represent the tile type (1-9). The 2 least significant bits represent the number of times (0-3) the tile is rotated clockwise by 90 degrees from the default position pictured above. Example: the starting tile

(7)in the example boards is rotated 3 times, so the generated number in base 2 is “0111 11” or 31 in base 10. On the final line of the input is a single symbol indicating the tile type that is available to play.

The starting tile (7) and ending tile (8) will always be in opposite corners. There will be (N + 1) / 2 objective tiles (9) that you must reach before going to the ending tile.

Output

Output to standard out all of the plays necessarily to reach the each of the objective tiles and finally to the ending tile. Each play should be one line of output. There are three possible plays available:

∙Push <location> <rotation>

∙Move <direction>

∙Collect

Where <location> is a number between 1 and (N-1) * 2, <rotation> is a number between 0 and 3, and <direction> is one of the following strings: “left”, “right”, “up”, or “down”.

Sample output for the sample boards pictured is available below.

Reminder: When you visit an objective tile, you must indicate that you are there by outputting the “collect” action.
